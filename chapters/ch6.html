Cooling Systems

6.1 Concept Selection

6.1.1 Criteria

Following Section 3’s Design Approach and Specifications, the Cooling Mechanism must ensure

  • uniform circumferential cooling of the PP filament along its circumference, and
  • an appropriate cooling rate to minimise cross-sectional temperature gradient,

while producing at least 100 g/hr of PCW-PP filament and ensuring the system remains benchtop-sized.

6.1.2 Water vs Air

Based on initial literature research, we have narrowed our options to two methods commonly used in filament production: water and air cooling. Considering PP’s low thermal conductivity (0.11 W/m.K) [43], rapid cooling will cause the skin to cool and crystallise much earlier than the core which causes ovaling of the filament. Additionally, slower cooling promotes crystallisation, thereby enhancing the filament's stiffness and preventing buckling during FFF/3D printing [77][78].

The maximum rate of cooling allowed is quantified by the Biot Number Bi. It is the ratio of internal thermal resistance to external convective thermal resistance:

Bi = HcLckPP

Where Lc is the characteristic length of the material, Hc is the fluid’s convective heat transfer coefficient, and kpp is the thermal conductivity of PP taken as a constant at 0.11 W/m.K [43][79]. When Bi < 1 the temperature gradient is sufficiently gradual such that differences in the skin and core’s crystallisation and shrinkage are small and will not cause ovaling.

Using data from literature research (Table 22), we calculated and compared the expected parameters required when using air and water to achieve the same cooling rates:

From Table 22, an extremely large range of air speeds can achieve our desired cooling rate without causing significant crystallisation and ovality. However, for the water bath to be feasible, the pull speed of the filament needs to be very slow, significantly slower than the specified production rate of 100 g/hr.

Therefore, we chose to use air for cooling, as it supports gradual cooling. In contrast, for the water bath, any modifications to ensure gradual cooling would be impractical or too complex, as they would involve longer water baths at high temperatures.

6.1.3 Fan Choice and Configuration

We explored various fan types and configurations to achieve our cooling criteria.

Table 23: Description of fan types

Axial Fan: Has higher air flow volume, but the air flow pattern swirls a lot. Causes undesirable fluttering of the filament as it stretches and thins the filament uncontrollably [88]. Centrifugal Fan: Has lower air flow volume, but air flow is direct with no swirls. Promotes a more stable airflow pattern around the filament, thereby minimising fluttering.

Table 24: Design of fan configuration

Single-axis Cooling, Perpendicular Airstream
 Fans blow perpendicular to filament from one side. Not chosen due to the high likelihood of ovality caused by highly asymmetric cooling.
Multi-axis Cooling, Perpendicular Airstream
 Fans blow perpendicular to the filament from more than two sides. Direct blowing allows for faster cooling. However, strong cross-winds cause the filament to flutter and stretch uncontrollably. Cooling length is also likely insufficient and would require more fans installed along the filament’s length, increasing the system's footprint.
Multi-axis Cooling, Parallel Airstream
 Fans blow parallel to the filament from more than two sides. The cooling rate is lower than with a perpendicular airstream, but fluttering is less likely to occur. Moreover, the cooling length is the longest for airflow parallel to the filament, reducing the need for additional fans.

We decided to go with Multi-axis Cooling and a Parallel Airstream to achieve the most stable and uniform circumferential cooling of the filament.

6.2.2 Effectiveness of Cooling

Subsequently, we analysed the cooling efficiency of the fans by setting the initial volumetric temperature of the PP filament at 180°C and running the same simulation for 30s, which is equivalent to ~40cm of filament length when pulled at 0.013 m/s.

The final average surface temperature of the filament was then measured at regular intervals along the filament (Fig 45). From which, we identified two issues:

  • The filament was still too soft as its temperature was still high, more than 85°C, and would likely deform between the rollers [89].
  • The temperature significantly increases down the filament (left to right) to a maximum of 161°C from 99°C (Fig 45).

To understand the two identified issues, we applied the Continuity Equation [86] to the fans:

ṁinlet = 𝑝AV = ṁoutlet

where ṁ is the mass flow rate of air, 𝑝 is the air density, A is the cross-sectional area (perpendicular to the air flow’s direction), and V is the air velocity. From the equation, the blown air will quickly disperse after exiting the centrifugal fan’s outlet (small A) into the environment (infinite A), causing its velocity and hence cooling effectiveness to drop significantly further down the filament’s length. Our simulations also agreed with this theory (Fig 46).

Therefore, to minimise the dispersion of air as it blows along the filament, a round wind tunnel was designed to contain and guide the air along the filament’s longitudinal axis, thereby extending the effective cooling zone (Fig 47).

From Fig 49 & 50, the Wind Tunnel is shown to both improve air flow velocity around the filament and significantly reduce the surface temperature of the PP filament down to a maximum of 81°C.

6.2.3 Thermal Analysis

To further optimise the Wind Tunnel’s 4-axis air cooling setup, we decided to study the correlation of the Wind Tunnel’s diameter with the filament’s final temperature. The simulation was run using diameters ranging from 50mm to 110mm, where any smaller would make laying the filament in the tunnel hard, and any larger would result in an exponentially smaller wind velocity following the Continuity Equation. The results are shown in Table 27 and Fig 51.

From Fig 51, a general trend was observed: the filament’s average surface temperature decreased as the diameter decreased. This was in agreement with the Continuity Equation, where at constant ṁ, the air’s velocity would increase and cool the filament down faster. The best cooling efficiency occurs at a diameter of 57.5mm with the lowest average surface temperature of 68.5°C.

However, an increase in temperature was observed from 57.5mm to 50mm in diameter. This is likely caused by a transition from a turbulent to a more laminar flow as the gap between the filament and the Wind Tunnel’s inner wall decreases, which reduces Hc [90]. Additionally, a drop in temperature occurred from 95mm to 110mm diameter, likely due to a transition to a more turbulent flow, which increases Hc. However, it is unlikely that increasing the diameter further would improve cooling, as the air would eventually lose too much speed and disperse as A increases.

6.3 Preliminary Prototyping

From previous experiments conducted using PP pellet feedstock, a shorter wind tunnel (20cm), and at a slower pull speed (0.0062 m/s) (Fig 52), we learned that wind speeds above 2.65 m/s or below 1 m/s can achieve the desired filament ovality of < 0.08mm (Fig 53). Additionally, wind speeds between 1.6 m/s and 3.7 m/s can achieve our desired diameter fluctuations of < 0.1mm (Fig 54). However, it was observed that at lower rates of cooling, the filament does not harden quickly enough and tends to sag under its own weight, causing uncontrolled stretching and thinning of the diameter, which was not captured in the graphs below.

Referencing the benchmarked cooling rate, we increased the filament pulling speed to 0.0128 m/s, to achieve our target PCW-PP filament production rate of 100 g/hr. Concurrently, due to the reduced time spent in cooling, we calculated a new wind speed of at least 4.6 m/s and a need to double the cooling length to achieve the same degree of cooling to ensure the complete solidification of the filament’s cross-section down to the core (Appendix H).

6.4 Omni-Flow

Following our simulation and iteration, we designed Omni-Flow, a Wind Tunnel equipped with a 4-axis Parallel Airstream, to efficiently and evenly cool PCW-PP filament and achieve the best cross-sectional circularity. It also features a ball-bearing roller to support the weight of the molten filament.

6.5 Results & Conclusion

In summary, it was identified that, amongst the evaluated components, the wind tunnel with the 4-axis Parallel Airstream produces the best cooling outcomes; hence, it was chosen as our key component for the Cooling Mechanism, and Omni-Flow was developed.
