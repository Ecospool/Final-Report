7.2 End-to-End Filament Quality Assessment

We developed an integrated prototype trial in which all selected parameters are applied simultaneously to test whether the integrated mechanisms could produce filament that achieved our target metrics of consistency.

7.2.1 Physical and Dimensional Metrics

As described in Section 2.2, three primary metrics were used to validate filament quality. For diameter consistency, a nominal diameter of 1.75mm with a maximum allowable tolerance of ± 0.10mm was adopted as the acceptance criterion. Ovality was required to remain below 5% of the nominal diameter, corresponding to an absolute threshold of ± 0.08mm. For surface defects, the acceptance criteria were defined as the absence of major defects across the sample, which are categorised as visible bumps.

7.2.2 System Parameters for Assessment

Using the identified optimised parameters of rPP and PCW-PP, and accounting for the minimum production rate of 100 g/hr, we conducted a full system integration test by extruding 250cm of filament. The extruded rPP and PCW-PP filament are then compared against each other.

7.2.3 Results and Discussion

From Figure 64 and 65, rPP’s ovality and standard deviation were within the threshold of < 0.08mm and < 0.1mm respectively above a wind speed of 4.8 m/s. This agrees with our preliminary findings mentioned in Section 6.3 and can be attributed to the optimisation of the fully integrated system to process PP flake feedstock.

However, PCW-PP’s ovality and standard deviation (Fig 64 and 65) were not kept within their respective thresholds, with the lowest mean ovality (0.092mm) and standard deviation (0.137mm) achieved at a wind speed of 4.9 m/s. This is likely a result of the variable chemical make up of the PCW-PP feedstock influencing the extrusion rate and shrinkage uniformity. Nevertheless, 4.9 m/s was identified as the optimised wind speed for the setup and used to plot the filaments’ diameters against their respective lengths.

Comparing the two filaments, rPP’s mean diameter is close to the ideal 1.75mm, and remains largely within the ideal diameter band of 1.85mm to 1.65mm, with a maximum diameter range of 0.34mm (Fig 66). PCW-PP’s mean diameter is further from the ideal at 1.63mm, and has a larger diameter deviations range of 0.61mm indicating large, random surging and thinning behaviours (Fig 67). These large fluctuations in diameter and the high ovality are likely induced by the non-uniform distribution of additives in PCW-PP, which affected the uniformity of the filament's material properties along its length. These were expected outcomes of utilising PCW feedstock, and it is to be determined if the PCW-PP filament can still be printed with.

7.2.4 Printability Testing

To assess the printability of the extruded PCW-PP filament, a 3DBenchy was printed using an Ender v2 Neo using a 0.4mm nozzle (Fig 68 and 69). The slicer settings for the print are found in Appendix H.

During printing, the print progressed without issue through the initial sections of the model, with consistent layer adhesion and acceptable surface finish despite signs of under-extrusion or slipping. However, the print was interrupted midway when a particularly thick portion of the filament entered the extruder, causing a jam and halting the print prematurely.

While this outcome precluded a complete Benchy print, the successfully printed portion nonetheless demonstrates that the filament exhibits adequate printability over consistent-diameter lengths. The jam is attributable to a localised diameter deviation rather than a fundamental material or rheological incompatibility. This is in part a consequence of Benchy's relatively large print volume: at 30mm × 16.5mm × 24mm, it consumes a substantially longer length of filament than smaller prints, increasing the likelihood of encountering an inconsistent-diameter section. This interpretation is further supported by the pilot test described in Section 8.3, where smaller architectural components (approximately 10mm × 10mm × 30mm) were printed to completion without interruption, as their smaller filament consumption reduced exposure to any diameter variations. Together, these results confirm that the PCW-PP filament can sustain functional prints when diameter consistency is maintained.

Improving diameter uniformity over longer extrusion runs is, therefore, expected to directly translate into more reliable print outcomes.

7.3 Comparison with Commercial Filament

7.3.1 Dimensional Stability

Reputable filament manufacturers such as Prusament [91] guarantee diameter tolerances of ±0.02mm, achieved through continuous dual-axis laser measurement during production. While our process employs dual-axis dial gauges rather than inline laser sensors, our measurements indicate that the extruded PCW-PP filament meets this dimensional benchmark over consistent sections of the produced length, demonstrating that our filament can achieve dimensional consistency comparable to commercial standards.

7.3.2 Thermal Performance and Warping Tendency

Commercial PP filaments commonly incorporate nucleating agents, compatibilisers, or fillers to mitigate the material's characteristically high shrinkage coefficient. The severe warpage induced by volumetric shrinkage is a well-known problem in semi-crystalline thermoplastics like PP, which exhibit high crystallinity due to their isotactic macromolecular structure [92]. Our PCW-PP filament, being free of these extrusion-enabling additives, is expected to exhibit greater warping tendency than commercial formulations, as observed during the pilot prints described in Section 7.4.

Accordingly, the acceptance threshold for PCW-PP filament in an educational institution context should be calibrated to functional print performance rather than high-precision dimensional tolerances. Suitable target applications include concept models, prototypes, and aesthetic components rather than dimensionally critical mechanical parts, where the warping tendency of unfilled PCW-PP would be a limiting factor.

7.4 Pilot Tests & Reviews

To validate end-use demand and assess the practical replaceability of PP filament in real fabrication workflows, two pilot print tests were conducted.

1. Architectural Model Components - Commercial PP Filament

In collaboration with a Year 5 architecture student, print files originally intended for fabrication in PLA were printed using commercially available PP filament under identical printer settings (Fig 70). The printed parts were evaluated for dimensional fidelity and structural adequacy and found satisfactory for their intended use in an architectural model.

2. Architectural Model Components - Extruded PCW-PP Filament

A further test print was conducted using the PCW-PP filament extruded by the prototype system for two same objects (Fig 71). The print was similarly assessed and found to be functionally satisfactory. Minor stringing and warping were observed; however, these artefacts were also present in prints produced from the commercial PP filament under the same settings, indicating that these issues are attributable to unoptimised print parameters rather than filament quality. With calibrated slicer settings, particularly retraction tuning, and an enclosed print environment to mitigate warping, print quality is expected to improve significantly.

The successful reproduction of the student's components in PP demonstrates two things: first, that PP is a viable substitute for PLA in desktop FFF printing for architectural modelling applications; and second, our produced PCW-PP filament addresses a real demand, validating that recycled PP waste from NUS laboratories can indeed be extruded into filament and reintroduced into the fabrication workflows of NUS students, closing a material loop within the university ecosystem. The remaining challenge is ensuring diameter consistency across the full length of a spool, as only once spool-level uniformity is achieved can the PCW-PP filament be reliably deployed end-to-end without risk of mid-print jams of the kind encountered in Section 7.2.4.
