Problem Analysis

Filament quality requirements, feedstock characterisation, and the two root-cause issues driving design decisions.

This chapter examines the inherent challenges of processing polypropylene (PP) into filament, a material known for its complex thermal and crystallization behaviour. To systematically uncover the underlying causes of inconsistent filament quality, we will (1) identify the fundamental factors that influence filament formation, (2) define clear and measurable quality metrics, and (3) highlight the key issues within the filamentation process that hinder their attainment (Fig 3). By tracing these issues back to their originating mechanisms, the chapter ultimately converges on the root causes, providing a logical and evidence-based foundation to inform the design approach in the subsequent section.

2.1 Filament Quality

Filament quality is defined by a set of measurable criteria that reflect its dimensional consistency, structural integrity, and printability [2][3]. Establishing the key metrics that constitute high-quality filament is essential to guide our design process. This will enable us to pinpoint the main challenges of filamentation and better understand the underlying factors that drive them.

In developing our system, these metrics are not treated with equal priority, but rather evaluated based on their direct impact on print reliability and process stability.

Filament diameter governs the volumetric consistency of material fed into the printer [3]. Sanchez et al. stated diameter is the most important quality parameter for the characterization of the filament produced, where even minor fluctuations in diameter translate directly into over- or under-extrusion, leading to defects such as clogging, poor layer adhesion, or dimensional inaccuracies in printed parts [8].

In contrast, while moisture content and melt flow index influence material behaviour and are important for process optimisation, their effects are secondary and can be difficult to measure and address at the early stages of prototyping. Surface finish, although relevant for end-use aesthetics, does not critically affect the fundamental functionality of the printing process [9].

Therefore, we established Filament Diameter as our primary focus; to ensure stable and consistent extrusion as a baseline requirement before addressing material refinement and aesthetic quality.

2.2 Metrics of Filament Diameter

Ideal filament diameter is defined by two parameters; a nominal diameter of 1.75mm and a perfectly circular cross-section [9]. These specifications are standardised across commercial 3D printers to ensure smooth feeding into the extruder and consistent, predictable flow through the nozzle [10][11].

In practice, filament does not adhere perfectly to these ideal specifications, and a certain degree of deviation is acceptable within defined tolerances. To quantify this deviation, two key industry metrics are used:

2.3 Feedstock Material

2.3.1 Post-Consumer Waste PP Flakes

Our target PP material is sourced primarily from pipette boxes used in NUS laboratories (Fig 5). For the purposes of this report, it will be referred to as post-consumer waste PP (PCW-PP).

Compared to conventional virgin PP used for filamentation, PCW-PP differs in one key aspect: purity.

The PCW-PP boxes are manufactured via injection moulding, which typically incorporates additives such as pigments, stabilisers, and fillers to modify the properties of the base polymer [13]. As such, the PCW-PP feedstock is not chemically pure PP but a composite material containing various additives. However, polypropylene still constitutes the bulk of the material, which is why such products are generally referred to simply as PP.

These additives introduce variability in the material’s behaviour during extrusion, like melt viscosity and flow characteristics, leading to inconsistencies in melt flow and pressure along the barrel [14]. This can disrupt melt-flow stability, contribute to fluctuations in extrusion rate, and ultimately lead to defects such as diameter inconsistency in the produced filament. The specific effects of additives will be further elaborated on in section 2.4.

2.3.2 Control Material: rPP Flakes

These impurities in the PCW-PP feedstock introduce additional complexity to the filamentation process beyond the inherent challenges of processing PP and handling irregular flake geometry. The variability in additive composition introduces a high degree of unpredictability in material behaviour [15][16], making it difficult to achieve process stability or to draw reliable conclusions on the effectiveness of individual mechanisms if experiments were conducted directly on composite PCW-PP flakes.

Rather than serving as a direct proxy for PCW-PP, this controlled material provides an idealised baseline that enables repeatable experimentation and isolates the fundamental challenges associated with (1) processing PP and (2) handling irregular flake feedstock. This allows the system to be developed and validated under well-defined conditions, establishing a proof of capability in addressing these two key design considerations. By optimising our system for pure PP as the dominant material, it is expected that the system will be sufficiently robust to accommodate the variability present in composite PCW-PP feedstocks..

This feedstock was produced by extruding filament from virgin PP pellets and flattening the extrudate between rollers before it fully cooled. The resulting material, when cut into individual pieces, forms pure rPP flakes that closely mimic the geometry and bulk density of the PCW-PP flake feedstock (Fig 6). After our rPP flake was produced, we tabulated the bulk densities to ensure similarity to our PCW-PP flake feedstock (Appendix B).

The bulk density of PCW-PP flake is 0.445 g/cm3 while the rPP flake is 0.459 g/cm3, verifying that the feedstocks are similar in geometry and bulk density.

This approach is acceptable because a single thermal cycle does not significantly affect the integrity of the polymer [17][18][19]; as such, the resulting rPP flakes retain properties comparable to virgin PP and can serve as a reliable proxy for evaluating and comparing the behaviour of composite PCW-PP flakes.

2.4 Key Issues

Achieving consistent filament diameter and minimising ovality presents significant control challenges, as any dimensional deviation is a symptom of underlying inconsistencies within the extrusion process. While most literature focuses on identifying key process controls to manage in the extrusion process, it is undeniable that these solutions are driven by the polymer’s inherent material properties [20][21][22].

Therefore, we narrowed down the priary issues (Fig 7) that affect the consistency of filament to:

  1. Diameter Variance arises from an Inconsistent Rate of Extrusion [19], and
  2. Ovality arises from Anisotropic Volumetric Shrinkage during cooling [23].

This section expands on these issues and systematically traces them back to their underlying root causes.

2.4.1 Issue 1: Inconsistent Rate of Extrusion

Inconsistent extrusion of filament results in portions of filament thickening and thinning above or below the ideal diameter, respectively. Since the filament is continuously drawn at a relatively constant puller speed, any fluctuation in the volumetric flow rate directly translates into a mismatch between material supply and take-up rate. When the extrusion rate increases, excess material accumulates, and the filament diameter increases; conversely, a reduced extrusion rate leads to insufficient material, causing the filament to stretch and thin (Fig 8).

These deviations occur at the point of extrusion, and are attributed to the inherent mechanical and chemical properties of post-consumer PP waste — melt flow instability and high complex viscosity of PP when using flakes (Fig 9) [24][25][26].

Root Problem 1-A: Melt Flow Instability

As discussed in Chapter 1.3, shredded PP flakes were selected as the feedstock to avoid the additional thermal cycle associated with pelletization, thereby minimising further thermal degradation and preserving the material’s properties and usable lifespan [27].

However, unlike pellets—which are manufactured with consistent size, shape, and density—flakes exhibit substantial variability in geometry and packing behaviour (Fig 10). This inconsistency leads to irregular feeding and compaction within the extruder at low temperature and pressure, which in turn disrupts melt homogeneity and compromises flow stability during extrusion [28][29].

Based on our feedstock samples (Appendix B), the bulk density of Virgin PP Pellet is 0.615 g/cm3 while that of PCW-PP flakes is 0.445 g/cm3.

The irregular geometry of flakes results in non-uniform bulk packing within the extruder feed zone, producing bulk density variation characterised by regions of dense packing interspersed with entrapped air and gas inclusions [30][31].

Irregular geometries lead to uneven heat transfer because variations in surface area and inter-particle contact disrupt consistent thermal conduction between flakes. As a result, the polymer does not melt uniformly along the barrel, producing regions that are either over- or under-heated [32]. This creates localised differences in viscosity: lower-viscosity regions flow more readily, while higher-viscosity regions resist flow, leading to non-uniform flow velocities within the melt.

These inconsistencies give rise to melt flow instability, characterised by fluctuations in density and flow rate throughout the extruder. Instead of a steady, homogeneous melt stream, the extrusion process becomes unsteady, leading to alternating periods of over- and under-extrusion. This ultimately manifests as variations in filament diameter.

When processing injection-moulded PCW-PP flakes, additives such as fillers (eg, talc, glass fibres) increase particle stiffness and reduce compressibility, making it more difficult for the screw to compact the material uniformly [33]. This aggravates bulk density variation, as loosely packed regions with trapped air coexist with highly compacted zones. Consequently, different regions of the melt will have varying thermal conductivities. This results in persistent pockets of uneven melting, where some regions are fully molten while others remain partially solid or highly viscous, further exacerbating melt flow instability.

Therefore, the irregular nature of the flake feedstock necessitates a heating and compression mechanism capable of consistently compacting, uniformly melting, and steadily conveying the material through the barrel to maintain a stable and controlled extrusion rate.

Root Problem 1-B: High Complex Viscosity

Complex viscosity refers to the effective resistance of a polymer melt to flow under dynamic deformation, accounting for both its viscous and elastic behaviour [34]. As this property is highly temperature-dependent, even small thermal variations can result in significant changes in viscosity, thereby strongly influencing melt flow behaviour during extrusion [35].

From Table 4, it can be seen that PP’s complex viscosity is about 5 and 60 times more sensitive to temperature changes than PET's and PLA's, respectively. Owing to its comparatively high complex viscosity, PP exhibits pronounced sensitivity to even minor thermal fluctuations during melting [36]. Consequently, small deviations in temperature can induce disproportionately large changes in viscosity. A minute increase in temperature can lead to a sudden decrease in viscosity, leading to increased flow rate and hence a surge in material being extruded. Conversely, a decrease in temperature can lead to underextrusion. The instability and unpredictability of melt flow behaviour necessitate tight temperature control during melting to prevent fluctuations in filament diameter.

This issue is further exacerbated when processing injection-moulded PCW-PP flakes that contain additives. The presence of additives transforms the material from a relatively well-defined polymer into a more complex composite system. Each additive influences the melt behaviour in different ways; for example, a filler like talc would increase the overall complex viscosity and make the melt disproportionately more viscous at lower temperatures while causing a sharp drop in viscosity at higher temperatures. Consequently, even under controlled processing conditions, small perturbations can produce irregular changes in flow behaviour, making the extrusion rate more difficult to regulate reliably.

2.4.2 Issue 2: Anisotropic Volumetric Shrinkage

Anisotropic volumetric shrinkage of plastic can occur during cooling, which leads to a differential in internal tensional forces that distorts the circular cross-section of the filament into an oval. This is extremely prevalent in the filamentation of PP, owing to its high crystallinity and low thermal conductivity (Fig 11).

Root Problem 2-A: High Crystallinity

Typically, plastics with low crystallinity (e.g., PET and PLA) comprise mostly amorphous regions where molecules are irregularly arranged [37]. These regions will have minimal shrinkage during cooling, resulting in minimal distortion to the filament’s shape. Therefore, these polymers are preferred choices for 3D printing applications — PLA is the most common 3D printing material, and PET is widely used for recycled filaments.

In comparison, PP is a semi-crystalline polymer with molecular chains that are relatively regular and symmetrical. As the filament cools from its molten state after extrusion, these chains progressively organise into ordered crystalline regions while the remaining material solidifies in an amorphous form, giving rise to a high degree of crystallinity (30–80%) [39]. Regions with higher crystallinity are more densely packed and therefore undergo greater volumetric shrinkage (1–3%) [38], while more amorphous regions shrink less (Fig 12). PP’s semi-crystalline nature inherently gives rise to differential shrinkage between crystalline and amorphous regions, making it naturally susceptible to anisotropic shrinkage.

This effect is further intensified under asymmetric cooling conditions. The windward side cools and solidifies earlier, forming a rigid shell, while the leeward side continues to cool and contract [43]. As this subsequent shrinkage is constrained by the already-hardened windward side, unbalanced residual stresses develop, amplifying the anisotropic shrinkage and distorting the initially circular filament into an oval cross-section.

Due to PP’s relatively high degree of crystallinity and associated volumetric shrinkage compared to polymers such as PLA and PET, these differential shrinkage effects are more pronounced. As a result, PP is more susceptible to anisotropic shrinkage and ovality, making precise and uniform cooling control critical for maintaining filament dimensional accuracy.

Anisotropic shrinkage is further exacerbated when processing PCW-PP flakes containing additives. Many common fillers (e.g. talc, glass fibres) introduce direction-dependent behaviour in the polymer due to the alignment of fillers during prior injection moulding. As a result, shrinkage is no longer uniform in all directions—contraction becomes lower along the filler alignment direction and higher perpendicular to it, producing anisotropic dimensional changes. Furthermore, additives will alter the crystallisation behaviour of PP. Certain additives act as nucleating agents, increasing the rate or degree of crystallinity in localised regions.

These effects compound the inherent anisotropic shrinkage of PP, making the filament more prone to oval cross-sections and dimensional inconsistencies.

Root Problem 2-B: Low Thermal Conductivity

PP, like most polymers, exhibits low thermal conductivity (~0.11 W/m·K), which limits the rate at which heat can be dissipated from the material during cooling. As a result, heat is removed much more rapidly from the filament surface than from its interior, establishing a pronounced radial cooling gradient between the outer skin and inner core [43].

This non-uniform cooling leads to a temporal lag in solidification: the outer skin solidifies and begins to shrink earlier, while the core remains molten or semi-molten for a longer duration. As the core subsequently cools, crystallises, and undergoes volumetric shrinkage, it contracts against an already rigid outer shell. This mismatch in the timing and magnitude of shrinkage generates internal tensile stresses (Fig 13; seen as white arrows), producing anisotropic volumetric contraction that manifests as distortion of the filament cross-section, such as ovality.

The presence of additives in PCW-PP such as talc or glass fibres can modify the bulk thermal conductivity of PP. By increasing the overall ability of the material to conduct heat, these fillers accelerate heat dissipation from the filament during cooling. This can reduce the time available for the polymer chains in the core to relax and crystallise uniformly, potentially shifting the balance of contraction between the filament’s core and outer skin. As a result, the internal stresses that arise from differential shrinkage can be amplified, making the filament more prone to ovality.

2.5 State of the Art

Having identified the fundamental issues and root causes that affect filament quality, it is necessary to examine how these challenges manifest within the filamentation process in practice. By reviewing current extrusion systems and their approaches to processing PP, we can assess their limitations and identify where they fall short. This not only reinforces our understanding of the challenges associated with PP, but also provides direction for developing a more effective design approach in the next section.

2.5.1 Typical System Architecture

As illustrated in Fig 14, the production of recycled plastic filament goes through three main processes: Melting, Cooling and Pulling.

Process 1: Melting
Plastic feedstock is conveyed by the extrusion mechanism into the heating mechanism, where it is melted into a continuous molten flow. The molten material is then pushed through the extrusion nozzle to form the filament.

Process 2: Cooling
The molten filament passes through the Cooling mechanism, where it cools and solidifies.

Process 3: Pulling
As the filament solidifies, it is pulled and stretched by the Pulling mechanism and wound onto the spool via the Spooling mechanism.

2.5.2 Existing Extruders

Despite the growing availability of desktop and laboratory-scale extrusion solutions, PP remains a less preferred feedstock for recycling due to the difficulty of reliably producing high-quality rPP filament.

To better understand these limitations, three small-scale filament extruders that publicly advertise their ability to process PP—3Devo’s Filament Maker TWO, Filabot’s EX6, and Felfil’s Evo (Fig 15)—were examined to evaluate why their mechanisms struggle to consistently produce quality PP filament.

Existing small-scale extrusion systems are predominantly designed around pelletised feedstock of more stable polymers and are not optimised for the processing demands of PP flake material. A review of current systems identified key mechanism parameters that perform inadequately under these conditions:

1. Screw Compression Ratio

Among the systems reviewed, 3Devo’s screw has the highest compression ratio at 2.25:1. However, this remains below the typical optimal value of approximately 2.8:1 for standard pelletised feedstock [47]. For flake feedstocks with irregular geometry and lower bulk density, an even higher compression ratio is generally required to achieve sufficient compaction into a continuous, pressurised melt [48]. As such, the compression ratios of all three systems are likely insufficient to address the melt-flow instability of PCW-PP flakes.

2. Heating Zones

3Devo and Filabot extruders utilise multiple heating zones (typically four) to establish a gradual thermal gradient, which is better suited to managing PP’s sensitivity to temperature arising from its high complex viscosity [49]. In contrast, Felfil’s extruder employs a single heating band, resulting in more abrupt temperature changes. This can induce sudden drops in viscosity, leading to unstable melt flow and extrusion surging.

3. Cooling Arrangement

Filabot and Felfil extruders employ single-direction (axial) cooling, while 3Devo incorporates a dual-fan configuration to improve airflow distribution. Despite this, cooling remains asymmetric, and 3Devo reports that issues such as ovality—arising from anisotropic shrinkage—still persist [50]. For a polymer such as PP with a significantly higher crystallinity, the asymmetric cooling will definitely result in high ovality.

Overall, existing small-scale extrusion systems do not adequately address the root causes of dimensional instability in PP filament. Limited thermal control leads to viscosity fluctuations and consequent melt flow instability, while insufficient screw compression fails to maintain a stable flow under PP’s high complex viscosity. In addition, non-uniform cooling allows anisotropic shrinkage—driven by PP’s high crystallinity—to persist, resulting in ovality.

These limitations highlight a gap between existing extrusion designs and the specific processing requirements of PP flake feedstock. Building on the root causes identified, the following section outlines a targeted design approach aimed at directly addressing the identified root causes to achieve consistent, high-quality filament production.
