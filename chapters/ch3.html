Design Methodology

3.1 Design Approach

From Chapter 2, we have identified the two key metrics of filament consistency — diameter variance and ovality— along with their corresponding key issues and root causes: High Complex Viscosity, Melt Flow Instability, High Crystallinity and Low Thermal Conductivity. To address these 4 root causes, we proposed 4 conceptual solutions (Fig 16).

3.1.1 Solution 1-A: Even Material Compression & Mixing

A solution of even material compression and effective mixing addresses the non-uniform viscosity and flow characteristics of PP flakes.

With more uniform compaction, the flakes are consolidated into a more consistent bulk density before and during melting. This eliminates voids and reduces air entrapment, ensuring that the material experiences a uniform pressure and thermal environment along the barrel [32]. The enhanced mixing also helps homogenise temperature and viscosity throughout the melt.

With consistent melt density and viscosity, the screw can deliver material at a steady volumetric rate rather than in pulses. This ultimately improves consistency of extrusion rate and reduces fluctuations in filament diameter.

3.1.2 Solution 1-B: Uniform Heating Gradient

A uniform and well-controlled temperature gradient directly addresses PP’s high viscosity sensitivity by eliminating abrupt thermal variations along the melt path [49].

When the material is heated gradually, it transitions from solid to melt in a controlled manner, allowing its viscosity to decrease smoothly rather than abruptly. This prevents regions of thermal inconsistency, which would otherwise produce viscosity gradients along the melt path, manifesting as fluctuations in extrusion output.

A uniform temperature gradient along the barrel allows the polymer to reach a consistent melt state before extrusion, ensuring a steady extrusion flow rate. This consistent flow eliminates pulsations or surges in material output, which are the primary causes of diameter fluctuations.

3.1.3 Solution 2-A: Uniform Cooling Coverage (Circumferential)

By implementing circumferential cooling, the filament is exposed to a uniform cooling environment from all directions. This ensures that heat is removed evenly across the entire outer surface, promoting symmetrical solidification and shrinkage. As a result, the filament maintains a more consistent circular cross-section and improved dimensional accuracy, which is critical for reliable downstream processes such as spooling and 3D printing.

3.1.4 Solution 2-B: Even Cross-Sectional Cooling Gradient (Cross-sectional)

Due to PP's relatively low thermal conductivity, a gradual cooling approach is needed to ensure an even cross-sectional cooling gradient. This means controlling the cooling process such that the temperature difference between the skin and core is minimised at any given time. Instead of forcing the outer layer to cool much faster than the core, the entire cross-section is allowed to cool more synchronously.

This synchronised cooling minimises differential shrinkage between the skin and core, reducing the likelihood of internal residual stresses that would otherwise distort the final filament geometry.

3.2 Design Statement

Based on the conceptual solutions discussed in section 3.1, it is clear that uniform heating, mixing, and cooling are crucial to minimising fluctuations in filament diameter and ovality. Hence, our design statement reflects these key principles/design objectives and our desired outcome.

Our project aims to design a system that can uniformly melt, extrude and cool PP to produce consistent rPP filament.

3.3 System Overview

With reference to the mechanisms in a typical extrusion system (2.5.1), our conceptual solutions can be achieved by focusing our design modifications on 3 specific mechanisms:
1. Heating Mechanism — Uniform Heating Gradient
2. Extrusion Mechanism — Even Material Compression & Mixing
3. Cooling Mechanism — Uniform Circumferential Cooling and Even Cross-Sectional Cooling Gradient

Hence, we will refer to these 3 mechanisms as our Key Mechanisms, upon which we will focus our design process (Fig 17).

Our Functional Block Diagram (fig 18) demonstrates how we have decomposed the overall function of rPP filamentation into 3 subfunctions: Melting, Cooling, and Pulling. Between subfunctions, several mechanisms work in tandem throughout the process of converting PP flakes into rPP filament.

Apart from the 3 Key Mechanisms, the two remaining mechanisms — Pulling and Spooling — are our means of performance verification and filament storage. They allow us to measure the filament's diameter and ovality in real time and make the necessary adjustments to meet our target specifications. The set-up for these two mechanisms will be discussed in section 3.5.1.

3.5 Experimentation Methodology

3.5.1 Data Measurement

The standard filament spooling system (Fig 19) we obtained used a single-axis diameter sensor that cannot capture asymmetric deformation (ovality), which is one of the key performance metrics we have targeted.

Ovality is calculated by measuring the filament’s diameter twice at one point, along perpendicular axes, and then finding the difference between the two readings.

Ovality = Maximum Diameter - Minimum Diameter (at one cross-section)

Hence, to accurately quantify the ovality of the extruded rPP filament, we obtained 2 diameter dial gauges and mounted them perpendicular to each other to simultaneously record inline filament diameter measurements along two perpendicular axes (Figure 20 and 21).

To ensure consistent and objective data extraction, the measurements were captured on video and subsequently processed using a computer vision tracking pipeline (further described in Appendix M). The system was developed to automatically identify and extract the numerical readings from each gauge at defined intervals, eliminating manual transcription errors. The extracted values were then compiled and plotted as a continuous diameter profile across the 2.5-metre sample length, from which the mean diameter, standard deviation, and ovality were calculated.

To improve reliability and account for run-to-run variation, experiments were conducted in triplets to ensure consistent, reproducible results. This allowed us to detect and minimise errors, assess data variability, and confirm that the observed effects were due to our parameter modifications rather than random chance or external noise.

The average of these three runs was then treated as the ground truth for that cooling configuration and used as the primary basis for comparison across all experiments.

3.5.2 Data Processing

For the raw values we collected from each experimental run, we computed the following metrics:

  • Average diameter: calculated as Di = d1 + d22 for each measurement point
  • Standard deviation of diameter: using the sample standard deviation formula

S = √ ∑(Di - D̄)2 n - 1
where Di is the average diameter at point i, D̄ is the overall mean diameter across all points, and n is the number of data points (20).

To quantify filament ovality, we computed:

  • Pointwise ovality: |d1 - d2| for each point
  • Mean ovality: average of all pointwise ovality values
  • Maximum ovality: the largest ovality value observed across the filament
