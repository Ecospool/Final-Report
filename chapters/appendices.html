Category Reason Explanation
Economic Low Market Value Recycled PP (rPP) often costs more to produce than virgin PP due to low oil prices and higher processing costs, making it economically unattractive.[5]
Technical Difficult to sort and identify PP’s density and appearance are close to PE, making it hard to separate using standard float-sink or optical sorting without advanced systems. [6]

Market Demand Few high-value applications for rPP Compared to PET (used in textiles) or HDPE (used in bottles), PP’s recycling market is small and inconsistent.[8]

Viable Methods for Handling PP Waste

To effectively address PP waste, our team explored and compared three viable methods for upcycling and reusing this material: sequestration, repurposing, and recycling. These methods were evaluated based on their potential for further reuse, which creates new value for the material.

Table 2: Possible Methods of Upcycling and Reusing PP

Method Ability for further reuse Explanation
Sequestration Low Sequestration refers to the permanent trapping of PP waste, typically by embedding it in infrastructure, such as using it as filler material in roads. While this method prevents the waste from ending up in landfills, it is a low-reusability option because once the material is embedded, it cannot be retrieved or repurposed for future use. Therefore, its potential to contribute to a circular economy is extremely limited.
Repurposing Medium Repurposing involves finding creative or practical uses for PP waste, such as converting it into art pieces, panels, or simple consumer products. This approach provides a second life for the material and helps divert waste from incineration or landfill. However, its moderate reusability means that while PP is given a new purpose, reuse is typically limited to one-off or low-volume applications. Furthermore, repurposed products often have limited functional applications, which reduces the long-term impact on material circulation.
Recycling High Recycling is the process of reprocessing PP waste into raw materials that can be used to create new products. This method stands out for its high reusability, as it enables PP to be transformed into a wide range of new products. The reprocessed material can be remade into different forms and used across various industries, contributing to a circular economy model where materials are kept in circulation. Given that PP can be repeatedly recycled into new products, this method offers the highest sustainability and long-term value creation potential.

Based on this comparison, recycling emerges as the most sustainable and value-generating approach for managing PP waste. Not only does it allow for the creation of new products, but it also promotes the circulation of materials, thereby contributing to a more sustainable, closed-loop system.

Therefore, focusing efforts on improving the infrastructure and processes for recycling PP is crucial for enhancing its reusability and maximising its potential as a valuable resource.

Evaluation of Potential Methods for PP Recycling

To further explore the possibilities for recycling PP waste, we narrowed it down to three potential directions for reusing this material within the recycling process: plastic sheets, pellets, and 3D printing filament.

Table 3: Potential Directions for Reusing PP

Direction Explanation
Plastic Sheets Plastic sheets are one of the simplest and most cost-effective methods for transforming PP waste into new material. This process involves melting and moulding PP waste into flat, flexible sheets for various applications, such as packaging, construction materials, and DIY projects.
Plastic Pellets Pelletising is a more advanced recycling process that transforms PP waste into small, uniform plastic pellets. These pellets can then be used as raw material for the production of new plastic products. This method allows PP to be reprocessed at a larger, industrial scale, providing greater flexibility for manufacturing new products from recycled material.
3D Printing Filament Recycling PP into 3D printing filament sits between the other two methods. This process transforms PP waste into usable filament that can be employed in 3D printers for educational and commercial purposes, such as prototyping, design, student projects and manufacturing plastic products.

In the metering zone (highest compression),

d = 22mm, n = 2rpm, h = 25-222

Shear rate in metering zone is thus π × 22 × 260 × 1.5 = 1.54s-1 (3 s.f)

Appendix L: Value Proposition and stakeholder value

Value Proposition & Novelty

Value Proposition Statement

For NUS SSU who are looking to recycle PP waste on campus, our product is a small scale plastic extrusion machine that can produce consistent, high-quality rPP filament made entirely from campus waste unlike small-scale recycling systems that struggle with extruding PP consistently.This project proposes a flake-to-filament extrusion system designed for deployment in design-maker spaces, enabling the local production of polypropylene (PP) filament while supporting a closed-loop material ecosystem.

By allowing filament to be produced directly from recycled PP5 flakes, the system reduces material waste, eliminates an unnecessary thermal processing step, and lowers the barrier for institutions to adopt sustainable additive manufacturing practices.

Stakeholder Value

Our system empowers students with access to non-conventional materials such as PP and recycled PP5, enabling hands-on exploration of sustainable manufacturing, material behaviour, and real-world recycling workflows beyond standard PLA and ABS.

For design-maker spaces and institutions, it reduces reliance on commercially sourced filament by converting internal campus plastic waste into usable material, reinforcing circular economy principles while lowering long-term material costs.

For educators and technicians, the modular platform serves as a practical teaching tool for extrusion fundamentals, process optimisation, and material–process relationships, supporting incremental learning and hands-on troubleshooting.
