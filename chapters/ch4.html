Compression Screw

Design rationale, theoretical analysis, and Maddock solidification experiments for screw selection.

4.1 Air-Gap Insulation and Thermal Stratification

Loose PP flakes form an insulating air-gap bed in the feed zone, starving the downstream melt zone of consistent heat transfer. Compression eliminates these voids and ensures intimate contact between material and heated barrel wall.

4.2 Rationale for Compression Screws

4.2.1 Elimination of Air

Higher compression ratio (CR ≥ 3:1) physically consolidates flake material, reducing bulk density variation from 30% to under 5% along the screw channel.

4.2.2 Shear-Induced Heating

Viscous dissipation analysis at 6.7 rpm yielded a contribution of less than 1 °C — confirming barrel heating dominates and shear heating plays a secondary role at these low screw speeds.

4.2.3 Forced Convection and Mixing

Screw rotation induces helical flow, improving radial homogenisation of the melt temperature profile — reducing hot/cold streaks that cause diameter variation.

4.3 Theoretical Limitations of Excessive Compression

Compression ratios above 4.5:1 risk over-pressurisation of the die, causing melt fracture and surface irregularities. A CR of 3.5:1 was selected as a balance between void elimination and die-pressure stability.

4.4 Screw Selection and Compression Ratio Testing

Three screws were evaluated: CR 2.5, 3.5, and 4.0. CR 3.5 produced the lowest diameter standard deviation across repeated 20-minute steady-state extrusion runs.

4.5 Experimental Methodology

4.5.1 Maddock Solidification Experiment

60 g white PP + 0.3 g black masterbatch (1:200 ratio) were extruded at setpoints 185/195/200 °C. The screw was arrested mid-run and the barrel quenched. Sectioning revealed the melt front position and verified melting zone boundaries.

4.5.2 Procedure & Key Findings

Black masterbatch tracer confirmed complete melting by Zone 3 entry for CR 3.5, but incomplete melting at Zone 2 mid-point for CR 2.5. This validated the three-zone setpoint design and compression ratio choice.

4.6 Results & Conclusion

The CR 3.5 compression screw reduced diameter standard deviation by 38% relative to the Felfil baseline screw. It was adopted as the primary screw for all subsequent experiments.
