Compression Screw

4.1 Air-Gap and Thermal Stratification

4.1.1 Criteria

Following Section 3’s Design Approach and Specifications (fig 22), the compression mechanism used must ensure:

  • even material compression of the PP flakes along the barrel length, and
  • uniform mixing and homogenisation of the melt prior to the metering zone

The primary challenge with PP flakes is their low bulk density and high void fraction, defined as the amount of air trapped between flakes.

In Fig 23, pellets are much easier to pack compared to the less densely packed flakes. Based on preliminary measurements (Appendix B) using a fixed-volume water displacement method, rPP flakes exhibited a bulk density of approximately 0.459g/cm³, compared to 0.615g/cm³ for PP pellets — a difference of approximately 20%, measured over a single trial.

This preliminary result is consistent with the expectation that irregular flake geometry has substantially more air gaps than that of pellet feedstock of equivalent polymer type.

With a non-compression screw commonly used in competing systems, flakes remain loose throughout the barrel. The packing density does not increase along the screw length; air gaps remain, and the screw fails to fully compress the material ahead of the metering zone [51]. This prevents the system from reaching full melt seal, leading to a condition known as thermal stratification — where there are significant temperature differentials between material in contact with the heated barrel wall and material at the core of the solid bed. Rather than melting uniformly, the feedstock develops inconsistent melt regions, ultimately manifesting as trapped air bubbles and surging in the extruded filament (Fig 24).

Without proper compression, leaving air gaps can lead to two other issues within the barrel:

  • Stagnant Layer Degradation: The plastic in direct contact with the barrel reaches the setpoint temperature quickly; if it is not being compressed or moved, it can become stuck to the wall and begin to degrade.
  • The "Cold Core”: As the centre of the screw remains significantly cooler than the barrel wall due to the air gaps present. In a non-compression screw, heat transfer is almost entirely conductive, but it must travel through air pockets. The melting of PP becomes inconsistent between areas closer to the barrel wall and screw [52].

A non-compression screw (Fig 25) lacks the mechanical pressure to force air out and induce mixing, hence it does not form a homogeneous fluid as intended.

4.2 Rationale for Compression Screws

A compression screw (Fig 26) solves the thermal stratification problem by transitioning from conductive heating to frictional (viscous) heating.

4.2.1. Elimination of the Air

As the channel depth decreases in a compression screw, air is physically forced back towards the hopper (venting). This ensures a solid-to-metal contact between PP and the barrel, allowing the heat to flow directly into the polymer without the insulating interference of air.

4.2.2. Shear-Induced Heating

A compression screw significantly increases the Shear Rate (̇𝛾’​) in the transition zone. Based on the law of viscous dissipation, the heat generated (Φ​) per unit volume through viscous shear is expressed as:

Φ = η(𝛾’)²

where η is the melt viscosity [53]. With a compression screw, the plastic undergoes self-heating through viscous dissipation, ensuring that the thermal energy is generated throughout the melt volume rather than conducted inward from the barrel wall alone [54]. This ensures that the core of the melt is as hot as the material in contact with the barrel. This improves the mixing of the PP flakes inside the barrel.

4.2.3 Forced Convection and Mixing

As PP flakes are squeezed, they are forced to move from the screw root to the barrel wall and back again. This mixing ensures a uniform melt.

A compression screw solves 2 main issues:

  • Improved Melting Consistency: Since PP requires high heat to melt. Compression ensures that as the material transitions from solid to melt, the decreasing volume maintains constant contact with the barrel wall, maximising conductive heat transfer (Fig 27 & 28).
  • Pressure Stabilisation: A high compression ratio creates a "melt seal", ensuring the melt is pressurised and free of surges before it reaches the die.

4.3 Theoretical Limitations of Excessive Compression

While a compression screw offers clear advantages in melting consistency and pressure stabilisation, there are theoretical limits beyond which increasing the compression ratio becomes counterproductive.

At excessively high compression ratios, the aggressive reduction in channel depth can mechanically compact the solid bed in the feed zone (Fig 29) before sufficient heat has been conducted into the material; this is known as a plugged feed zone. The theoretical basis for this limitation is expressed through the melting rate equation [56].

ω = Φ * X1/2

The melting rate ω is proportional to the square root of the solid bed width X in contact with the barrel wall. Since the compression rate strongly governs the solid bed width in a conventional screw [54], an excessively high compression ratio can prematurely reduce X, causing the melting rate to decline before the material has fully transitioned to a melt state. The solid bed consequently survives further downstream than intended, potentially entering the metering zone in a partially unmelted state.

This relationship is consistent with the viscous dissipation equation introduced in Section 4.2, where Φ = η(𝛾’)². The viscous dissipation term appears explicitly within the composite parameter Φ of the melting rate equation as:

(μ2)Vj²

This confirms that shear-induced heating is a driver of the overall melting rate. As the solid bed is mechanically compacted before melting, this actually reduces the available melt film thickness for viscous shear. With no proper melt film, (μ2)Vj² reduces, and there is not enough melt fluid to generate shear heat. X is prematurely crushed to near zero, so ω = Φ * X1/2 also drops.

Under these conditions, the screw acts against a compacted solid mass rather than a developing melt, resulting in elevated back pressure and inconsistent mixing. Rather than producing a homogeneous melt, the material exhibits pressure fluctuations that manifest as diameter inconsistencies in the extruded filament.

4.4 Screw Selection and Compression Ratio Testing

Literature indicates that for standard pellet feedstocks such as LLDPE, the optimal compression ratio is around 2.8, with ratios above this threshold associated with diminishing returns in melt quality and an increased risk of solid bed survival and flight wear [57]. However, this assumes a feedstock with a relatively high and consistent bulk density, which does not apply to PP flakes.

Flakes, being irregular in geometry and lower in bulk density than pellets, introduce substantially more void space into the feed zone. A compression ratio calibrated for pellets would be insufficient to consolidate this feedstock into a continuous, pressurised melt. As noted by DrDFlo [48], low bulk density materials inherently require a higher compression ratio to compensate for the additional void volume, and the compression section should taper gradually rather than steeply to allow sufficient time for thermal energy to penetrate the material before it is fully compacted.

Literature identified compression ratios in the range of 3.5:1 to 4:1 as suitable for PP flake processing [48], with 3:1 established as the lower bound below which flake processing would be insufficient [57]. With that, two screws were acquired for testing:

  • 3.5:1 compression ratio
  • 4:1 compression ratio

The objective was to identify the point at which increasing compression yields improved melting and homogenisation, and the point at which it begins to produce the counterproductive effects described in Section 4.3 — namely the mechanical compaction of the solid bed ahead of full melting.

4.5 Experimental Methodology

We executed 2 experiments varying screw compression ratios to evaluate the mixing efficiency of the screws in achieving uniform and well-mixed PP.

4.5.1 Maddock solidification experiment

We utilised a Maddock Solidification Experiment—an industry standard for visualising the internal transition of a polymer from a solid bed to a homogenised melt. Beyond its application here, the experiment also provides visual evidence that informs the subsequent heating analysis, as the melt distribution observed across the screw channel reflects the thermal conditions experienced by the feedstock along the barrel length (fig 30).

4.5.2 Procedure

The experimental procedure is conducted where a coloured masterbatch is introduced into a steady-state extrusion run to visualise the internal melting and mixing behaviour within the screw channel. In the original method, a natural resin serves as the base material and a coloured masterbatch is added at a ratio equivalent to 200:1 [58]. In our experiment, the natural resin was substituted with our feedstock as the base, and the coloured masterbatch was replaced with black dye — yielding a prepared mixture of 60g of rPP flakes combined with 0.3g of black dye.

Before introducing the mixture, the system was purged of any previous material until the extrudate appeared stable and consistent, ensuring that any observed colour variation in the results could be attributed solely to the experimental mixture rather than residual contamination. Once a steady extrusion flow was established, the feedstock–dye mixture was fed into the hopper. The output was monitored continuously, and as soon as the coloured material appeared at the nozzle, the machine was immediately shut down. The nozzle was then opened to observe the internal melt distribution and mixing state within the screw channel.

We experimented with 2 different feedstock types: rPP Flakes and PCW-PP Flakes, as seen in Fig 31.

A well-mixed sample would exhibit uniform dark coloration throughout the screw channel, particularly towards the metering zone. Conversely, a poorly mixed sample would present visible dye specks, patchy coloration, or translucent unmelted portions — indicative of incomplete homogenisation and solid bed survival.

4.6 Results & Conclusion

Based on visual inspection of the extracted solid mix, the 3.5:1 compression ratio produced the most favourable mixing quality, yielding a homogeneous melt distribution across the entire screw channel. This outcome was consistent with previously established theoretical expectations from a literature review and was validated experimentally. The sample exhibited notably denser packing of flakes at the leading edge of each screw flight, indicating that air was being progressively displaced and vented toward the feed end of the barrel — confirming the melt seal described in Section 4.2.

Conversely, the 4:1 compression ratio exhibited symptoms consistent with the plugged feed zone condition described in Section 4.3. The aggressive channel-depth reduction mechanically compacted the solid bed before sufficient thermal energy had been conducted into the material. Referencing ω = ΦX1/2 [54][59], prematurely reducing the solid bed width X will thereby suppress the melting rate. Rather than transitioning through thermally driven melting, where viscous shear in the melt film progressively liquefies the solid bed, the dominant mechanism shifted toward mechanical compaction of a partially solid plug. This resulted in elevated resistance and inconsistent melt distribution, with the solid bed surviving further downstream than intended before full homogenisation could be achieved.

Additionally, feedstock sourced from different material origins exhibited variation in processing behaviour under identical screw parameters, highlighting the inherent variability of PCW-PP waste. Differences in prior thermal history, contamination level, and geometric consistency across batches influenced melt characteristics, suggesting that the optimal compression ratio window identified here should be interpreted as feedstock-specific rather than universally applicable. Taken together, these results confirm that an optimal compression ratio window exists for PP flake feedstock, beyond which the benefits of increased compression plateau, while the risk of incomplete melting rises.
