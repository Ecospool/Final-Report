Conclusion

EcoSpool demonstrates that laboratory-scale polypropylene waste can be converted into printable 3D printing filament using a modified single-screw extruder, without intermediate pelletisation. The three primary design interventions — compression screw (CR 3.5), three-zone barrel heating (185-195-200°C), and Omni-Flow annular cooling — each delivered measurable improvements in diameter consistency and ovality.

The integrated prototype achieved a mean diameter of 1.77 mm with σ = 0.183 mm and demonstrated printability on a commercial FDM printer. Pilot user testing with NUS student engineering teams confirmed viability for non-structural prototyping applications.

The project establishes a reproducible, low-cost template for institutional PP recovery that can be adopted by other universities and small fabrication labs, contributing to localised circular plastic economy goals at campus scale.
