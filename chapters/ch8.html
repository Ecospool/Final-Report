Limitations & Future Work

8.1 Spool-Level Diameter Uniformity

The most pressing limitation is diameter inconsistency over long extrusion runs. As shown in Section 8.1.2, localised diameter variations are sufficient to cause mid-print jams, limiting the usable filament length. Continued parameter optimisation for PCW-PP and closed-loop diameter feedback control will enable spool-level uniformity.

8.2 Feedstock Contamination

Solid contaminants in the PCW-PP feedstock caused intermittent surging during extrusion, producing periodic diameter spikes in the extrudate (Fig 73).

Upstream sieving, a finer breaker plate and screen pack at the die entry, and a standardised washing and drying protocol for NUS laboratory waste would collectively reduce contamination-driven variability.

8.3 Scaling Throughput

The prototype produces filament at a rate sufficient for validation but not for maintaining a standing stock. If it were to supply filament to NUS fabrication workflows on an ongoing basis, higher screw speeds, larger-scale cooling configuration and motorised spooling with tension feedback would be necessary to support continuous operation.

8.4 Advanced Screw and Mixing Designs

The single-screw configuration provides limited dispersive mixing - a drawback when processing heterogeneous waste batches. Retrofitting a Maddock mixing section or barrier screw would improve melt homogeneity, thereby reducing localised viscosity variations that manifest as diameter fluctuations in the extrudate. Given that the Maddock solidification experiment was already conducted as part of this project, the groundwork for evaluating such designs is already in place.
