Heating & Melting Mechanisms

Barrel heating architecture, heater technology selection, screw-induced heating, and temperature zone setpoint derivation.

5.1 Barrel Heating

5.1.1 Single-Zone vs Multi-Zone Heating

Single-zone control cannot accommodate the distinct thermal requirements of the feed (preheat), compression (melt), and metering (die-approach) regions. Three independent PID zones were implemented to provide granular setpoint control.

5.1.2 Functional Roles of Each Heating Zone

Zone 1 (185 °C) preheats and begins softening; Zone 2 (195 °C) drives full plastication across the compression section; Zone 3 (200 °C) maintains melt fluidity and homogenises temperature before the die.

5.1.3 Thermal Limitations of Barrel Conduction

Barrel wall conductivity limits the radial heat penetration rate. At low screw speeds (2 rpm), residence time is sufficient for full plastication. At 6.7 rpm, screw-induced mixing supplements conduction.

5.2 Barrel Heater Concept Selection

5.2.1 Overview of Heating Technologies

Candidates evaluated: band heaters (resistive), ceramic heaters, cartridge heaters, and induction heating. Assessment criteria: thermal uniformity, controllability, cost, and retrofitability to the Felfil barrel.

5.2.2 Selection of Heating Method

Mica band heaters were selected: highest contact uniformity over the cylindrical barrel, compatible with standard SSR/PID control, and lowest cost. Induction heating was excluded due to barrel material incompatibility.

5.3 Screw-Induced Heating

5.3.1 Viscous Dissipation (Shear Heating)

Shear rates: 0.372 s⁻¹ (feed zone), 1.54 s⁻¹ (metering zone). With η₀ = 4,400 Pa·s, viscous dissipation contributes less than 1 °C at 6.7 rpm — confirming barrel heating dominates throughout the operating range.

5.3.2 Solid-Bed Friction

Before the melt front, frictional heat from solid-bed compaction contributes a secondary heating mechanism that accelerates onset of plastication near Zone 2.

5.4 Temperature Zone Setpoint Selection

5.4.1 Literature-Derived Starting Points

PP melting onset occurs at 165–170 °C; processing temperatures in the range 190–220 °C are reported for extrusion-grade PP. Initial setpoints were derived from these bounds and refined experimentally.

5.4.2 Experimental Methodology

Systematic variation of Zone 1–3 setpoints across 185–200 °C range. Each configuration run for 20 minutes at steady state; diameter metrics recorded at 1 Hz. The Tadmor melting model informed the zone boundary locations.

5.4.3 Experiment Results & Discussion

Configurations below 185 °C at Zone 1 showed incomplete melting (melt fracture, rough surface). Configurations above 205 °C at Zone 3 caused die drool and diameter sag. The 185/195/200 °C profile produced the lowest σ across all test runs.

5.4.4 Final Setpoint Selection

Zone 1: 185 °C · Zone 2: 195 °C · Zone 3: 200 °C. Validated by Maddock sectioning (§4.5.1) confirming complete plastication by Zone 3 entry at this profile.

5.5 Thermal Precision Requirements and Dimensional Accuracy

5.5.1 Viscosity Sensitivity to Temperature

PP melt viscosity is highly temperature-sensitive: a 5 °C increase reduces η by ~20%, directly affecting die throughput and filament diameter. PID control with ±1 °C band is necessary for diameter stability.

5.5.2 Pressure Variation and Filament Diameter Impact

Die pressure oscillations of ±5 kPa produce diameter swings of approximately ±0.08 mm — just within the acceptance criterion. Stable temperature is the primary lever for pressure stability.

5.5.3 Implications for Zonal Heating Control

Each zone requires independent SSR-based PID control with type-K thermocouples. Cross-zone thermal crosstalk was measured and found to be negligible at the 160 mm zone spacing on the Felfil barrel.

5.6 Results & Conclusion

The three-zone 185/195/200 °C heating profile with mica band heaters and PID control achieved sustained ±1 °C thermal stability. This was sufficient to maintain filament diameter within the ±0.1 mm acceptance criterion under steady-state conditions.
