Heating & Melting Mechanisms

Established Criteria for Heating Mechanism:

Following Section 3’s Design Approach and Specifications, the Heating Mechanism must ensure a uniform heating gradient across the length of the extruded filament, minimising spatial viscosity variation arising from PP's high complex viscosity sensitivity to temperature and thereby minimising diameter variance.

In single-screw extrusion, heat delivery to the polymer arises from two distinct sources: conduction through the heated barrel wall and mechanical (shear) heating generated by screw rotation. The relative contribution of each depends on system parameters such as screw speed, barrel geometry, and material properties. Together, these mechanisms must be carefully managed to achieve the thermal precision necessary for consistent filament output.

5.1 Barrel Heating

Using a generic extruder set-up as our baseline (Appendix D), we evaluated the Heating & Melting Mechanism.

The thermal management of the extruder barrel is a critical determinant of both process stability and the final mechanical properties of rPP filament. Barrel heating conducts heat inward through the steel barrel wall (thermal conductivity ≈ 37.67 W/m·K [60]) to establish a thermal gradient from the barrel inner surface into the polymer melt.

5.1.1 Single-Zone vs Multi-Zone Heating

While a single uniform heating zone may appear simpler for small-scale extruders, it presents significant operational risks. Most notably, if the entire barrel length is maintained at the peak melting temperature, PP pellets in the feed section can melt prematurely and melt together or adhere to the screw flights or barrel wall before they are properly conveyed - a phenomenon known as 'feed throat bridging'. This creates a blockage that jams the intake, halting the intake of new material. Pepelnjak et al. [61] states that maintaining a cooler feed zone is essential for maximising mass flux and ensuring material is 'gripped' effectively by the screw's mechanical action.

Multiple independently controlled heating zones, by contrast, provide a staggered energy input that aligns with the polymer's physical state changes as it transitions from solid pellets to a pressurised melt. A single barrel setpoint cannot supply sufficient energy ahead of this transition while simultaneously preventing overheating downstream; independently controlled zones resolve this by matching thermal input to the polymer's evolving state along the screw [49].

5.1.2 Functional Roles of Each Heating Zone

Multi-zone heating is typically executed across three distinct functional zones of the barrel:

  • Feed zone - ensures uniform intake and conveyance of solid pellets from the hopper
  • Compression/transition zone - compresses the polymer to reduce air and gas pockets that may form during feeding
  • Metering zone - homogenises the melt to ensure a uniform temperature and consistent head pressure for the die

5.1.3 Thermal Limitations of Barrel Conduction

Effective heat transfer into the bulk of the melt is significantly limited by the inherently low thermal conductivity of polypropylene (0.11–0.22 W/m·K [62][63]), which makes the melt behave as a thermal insulator. As a result, heat supplied by the barrel does not penetrate efficiently into the screw root, leading to radial temperature stratification: the melt near the barrel wall reaches the process temperature readily, while material near the screw root remains comparatively cooler.

This non-uniform temperature distribution across the melt cross-section is a known challenge in single-screw extrusion of polyolefins [64]. In the present system, where the barrel length is 0.40 m and screw diameter is 0.025 m (L:D = 17.6), the short residence time further constrains the opportunity for thermal equilibration across the melt cross-section.

5.2 Barrel Heater Concept Selection

5.2.1 Overview of Heating Technologies

The extrusion process requires a stable, controllable thermal input to transition the polymer from a solid granule to a homogeneous melt. In barrel heating, energy can be transferred via conduction (resistance heating), convection (fluid media), or electromagnetic induction.

Our extruder’s heating system must provide enough thermal energy to reach the melting point of PP (160°C - 170°C) [65] without overshooting, so as to prevent thermal degradation, which occurs at around 250°C [66].

5.2.2 Selection of Heating Method

Referring to the morphological chart in section 3.3, five heating methods are considered, which differ substantially in their heat transfer mechanisms, physical complexity, and, as a result, suitability for our compact extruder.

We selected electric resistance band heaters as our barrel heating technology on the following bases:

  • Multi-zone thermal profiling: As mentioned in 5.1.1, a single screw extruder requires distinct temperature setpoints across the feed, transition and metering zones. Band heaters can be stacked axially in discrete zones, each driven by a thermocouple and PID controller (Fig 36), enabling the graduated temperature ramp that PP requires. This zoned architecture is straightforwardly achievable with band heaters, unlike single-zone cartridge heaters, which require a complete barrel redesign.
  • Suitability for experiments: Band heaters clamp directly onto the barrel diameter and are secured with a fastener and screw. This is extremely useful for our experiment logistics when we disassemble the extruder for inspection of the screw during our Maddock experiments. The absence of fluid circuits (ruling out oil jackets) and high-voltage power electronics (ruling out induction inverters) substantially reduces integration risk.

While band heaters have limitations, namely their tendency to radiate heat to the surrounding environment, rockwool overwraps can be added around the bands to partially mitigate heat loss (Fig 37).

5.3 Screw-Induced Heating

While barrel conduction heats the polymer from the outside in, the rotating screw contributes heat through two internal mechanisms: viscous dissipation and solid-bed friction. Together, these provide heat closer to the screw root, partially compensating for the radial temperature stratification described in Section 5.1.3. In the present system (L = 0.40 m, D = 0.025 m, N = 2 RPM), the low screw speed means that barrel conduction serves as the primary heat source, with screw-induced heating playing a secondary role.

5.3.1 Viscous Dissipation (Shear Heating)

Viscous dissipation, also termed shear heating, arises from the conversion of mechanical energy into thermal energy as the polymer is sheared between the screw flights and the barrel wall. The rate of heat generated is proportional to the local shear rate and melt viscosity, and is therefore most significant in the compression zone where the screw channel depth decreases, and shear stresses are highest.

The shear rate is calculated as:

Shear rate = πdn60h [67]

where d is the screw diameter, n is the screw speed, and h is the channel depth. Using this equation, the shear rate at the feed zone (nearest the hopper) is calculated as 0.372 s⁻¹, and the shear rate at the metering zone (furthest from the hopper) is 1.54 s⁻¹.

By applying the Carreau model to the calculated shear rates, it was determined that the polypropylene operates deep within the Newtonian plateau region, with apparent viscosities peaking at 10,988 Pa⋅s in the feed zone. Despite this high viscous resistance, the calculated Brinkman numbers (Br < 0.02) confirm that the extrusion process is almost entirely conduction-dominated. This ensures exceptional thermal precision, as the contribution of internal viscous dissipation to the total energy balance is negligible at the low operational screw speed of 2 RPM. The calculations for shear rate, peripheral viscosity, apparent viscosity, and the Brinkman Number are presented in Appendix E.

5.3.2 Solid-Bed Friction

Solid-bed friction contributes during the earlier stages of melting, prior to full plasticisation, where solid polymer pellets or granules are dragged along the barrel wall and screw surfaces. The frictional contact between solid particles and metal surfaces generates heat directly at the solid-melt interface, accelerating the onset of melting in the feed and early compression zones [68]. This mechanism diminishes as the solid bed breaks down and the material transitions fully into the melt phase.

5.3.3 Combined Effect: Melt Film Formation and Radial Homogenisation

Classical melting theory describes the combined outcome of viscous dissipation and solid-bed friction as the formation of a thin melt film at the barrel wall, which is continuously sheared, redistributed, and renewed by the screw [68]. This circulatory melt flow enhances mixing and promotes more homogeneous heat distribution than barrel conduction alone could achieve, particularly in the radial direction, where PP's low thermal conductivity otherwise limits heat transfer.

5.4 Temperature Zone Setpoint Selection

5.4.1 Literature-Derived Starting Points

The selection of initial barrel zone temperature setpoints was guided by established processing ranges for PP. PP is semi-crystalline, with melting temperature typically in the range of 160–170°C, while recommended processing temperatures generally lie between 180–230°C, depending on grade, shear conditions, and application [69].

For the feed zone, it is recommended that the temperature be set to the melting temperature plus about 25°C [70]. For our setup, this gives a target of approximately 160°C + 25°C = 185°C. This initiates softening of incoming pellets while preserving solid-bed integrity for effective solids conveying along the screw flights.

For the compression zone, the temperature is typically increased relative to the feed zone to promote progressive melting and homogenisation of the polymer. This stage continuously forms a thin melt film at the barrel wall, which is sheared and redistributed into the bulk material. As such, sufficient thermal input is required to sustain this melt film formation while avoiding excessive temperatures that may lead to thermal degradation. Crabtree et al. [70] recommend a compression zone setpoint approximately 10°C above the feed zone as a starting point, yielding a target of approximately 195°C for the compression zone.

The metering zone subjects the now largely molten polymer to continued shear and mixing to achieve thermal and compositional homogenisation before discharge [71]. The metering zone setpoint is set at 200°C, marginally above the compression zone, to account for any residual unmelted material and to maintain melt fluidity at the die entrance.

5.4.2 Experimental Methodology

The suitability of the selected temperature profile (185°C / 195°C / 200°C) was evaluated through a combination of qualitative melt assessment and quantitative filament dimensional analysis under controlled operating conditions.

Melt quality was first assessed using the Maddock solidification experiment conducted in Section 4.5.1, using a screw speed of approximately 2 RPM and a compression screw ratio of 3.5:1 (determined to be the optimal one from Section 4.5.1 that would promote uniform melting while avoiding premature mechanical compaction of the solid bed). The uniform grey colouration observed along the screw channel (Fig 32) indicates complete dispersion of the dye within the polymer melt. This is indicative of adequate melting, as distributive mixing of the dye into the PP mix can only occur once the polymer has transitioned from a solid bed to a homogeneous melt - solid-state mixing alone is insufficient to produce uniform colouration at this scale.

Extruded filament was subsequently inspected for visual indicators of thermal imbalance. Signs of under-heating, such as unmelted particles, surface roughness, or air entrapment (bubbles), were not observed. Similarly, indicators of overheating, including material discolouration, degradation, or charring, were absent. These observations collectively suggest that the selected temperature profile lies within an appropriate thermal processing window for PP.

To quantitatively evaluate process stability, filament diameter consistency was measured during a 2-minute continuous extrusion run under steady-state conditions.

All experiments were conducted under the following fixed operating parameters: a compression ratio of 3.5:1 (as determined to be optimal), screw speed of 2 RPM and puller speed of 1.85 as controlled variables, and wind speed set to its highest setting of 5.2m/s, so as to achieve quickest cooling allowable with our configuration. Within each feedstock set, the temperature profile was varied systematically while all other parameters were held constant, isolating temperature as the independent variable.

5.4.3 Experiment Results & Discussion

For a 2.5-meter length of filament derived from each experimental run, filament diameter was measured continuously using 2 dial gauges placed orthogonally. At every 3cm interval, the horizontal and vertical readings were averaged to yield a single diameter value, from which the mean and standard deviation were computed. Diameter standard deviation was used as the primary indicator of melt flow consistency: while absolute diameter and ovality are jointly influenced by factors such as cooling, temporal variation in cross-sectional area (calculated from diameter) reflects fluctuations in volumetric output at the die, which are primarily governed by the upstream heating profile. A higher standard deviation therefore indicates surging and/or under-extrusion, while a lower value reflects more uniform melt output.

We cut up sections of the filament we produced and verified that the entire cross-section was solid and that there were no air gaps / voids. This allowed us to confidently use the measured diameter values to calculate cross-sectional area.

PCW-PP flakes:

PCW-PP flakes represent the target feedstock of this project — post-consumer PP recovered from NUS laboratory waste streams. Unlike the rPP flakes, PCW-PP likely contains residual additives, contaminants, and a broader molecular weight distribution arising from prior thermal histories, all of which can substantially alter melt rheology relative to virgin PP [74].

When processed at 185–195–200°C (the optimal profile established for rPP flakes), the PCW-PP melt exhibited markedly lower viscosity than anticipated, producing a highly fluid extrudate that sags immediately upon exiting the die and could not be properly threaded through the cooling tunnel or spooler without eventually slipping off. This behaviour is consistent with viscosity reduction from either processing additives or the presence of contaminants [74].

5.4.4 Final Setpoint Selection

Based on the experimental results, the temperature profile of 155–165–170°C is selected as the operating setpoint to be carried forward into integration testing. This profile demonstrated superior dimensional consistency for PCW-PP flakes, producing lower diameter standard deviation relative to 145–155–160°C, indicating more stable melt flow and reduced surging. The higher absolute temperatures in this profile are sufficient to fully plasticise the PCW-PP feedstock while maintaining a melt viscosity high enough to draw the extrudate through the cooling tunnel and spooler without sagging.

5.5 Thermal Precision Requirements and Dimensional Accuracy

The strictness of temperature control is governed by PP's high sensitivity to temperature fluctuations and the consequences of viscosity variation on filament diameter consistency.

5.5.1 Viscosity Sensitivity to Temperature

In the event of a systemic fluctuation, such as an unintended temperature drop in the metering section caused by ambient heat loss through the nozzle, the impact on material properties is immediate. As the temperature falls below the setpoint, the thermal energy available to polymer molecules decreases, restricting their mobility and increasing the material’s viscosity [75].

This increase in viscosity has two primary consequences:

  • Increased drag flow resistance - more mechanical force is required to extrude the material
  • Increased pressure flow [76] - more pressure is required for material to flow through the die, creating a 'surging' effect

5.5.2 Pressure Variation and Filament Diameter Impact

To quantify the temperature stability required, the Arrhenius relationship for polymer melt viscosity is applied:

η = K · e^(Ea / RT)

The thermal stability of the metering zone is critical, given the PP output's high sensitivity to pressure fluctuations. For a material like PP, variations in die pressure are particularly disruptive: pressure variations can result in up to 3 times as much variation in total output [76].

At the operating temperature of 200°C (473.15 K), the Arrhenius relationship for recycled PP (Eₐ = 55 kJ/mol) indicates that a temperature deviation of 1.25°C produces a 3.61% change in melt viscosity. Since melt viscosity governs resistance to flow at fixed die geometry, this viscosity change is treated as an equivalent pressure variation. Referring to Dynisco’s sensitivity table, a 3.61% effective pressure variation corresponds to approximately 10.7% variation in volumetric output.

The acceptable output variation limit is derived from the filament diameter specification of 1.75 ± 0.10mm. The minimum acceptable cross-sectional area relative to the target is (1.65²/1.75²) × 100 = 88.9%, corresponding to a maximum allowable volumetric output variation of 11.1%. Since the calculated output variation of 10.7% from a 1.25°C deviation falls within this 11.1% limit, the PID control system must maintain the zone temperatures within ±1.25°C of their setpoints to ensure consistent extrusion of filament.

5.5.3 Implications for Zonal Heating Control

Consequently, the heating system must be tuned to a stability limit of ±1.25°C. Without multiple independent heating zones to isolate the metering section from the high-load transition zone, the PID controller would be unable to maintain this precision. The result would be decreased maximum output and inconsistent filament diameter beyond acceptable limits. Temperature gradients along the barrel are therefore a functional necessity for achieving dimensional accuracy in rPP filament production.

This requirement is reflected in the experimental performance of the system. During operation, the multi-zone PID temperature controller maintained each barrel zone within a narrow fluctuation band around its setpoint, as observed in real-time controller readings. The stability of the thermal profile is consistent with the measured filament diameter results in Section 5.4.3, where most of the extrudate displayed low standard deviation in cross-sectional area across the length of the filament, suggesting minimal surging and underextrusion. This demonstrates that the selected PID tuning parameters and multi-zone heating configuration are sufficient to achieve both thermal stability and dimensional consistency in practice.

5.6 Results & Conclusion

In summary, it was identified that Multi-Zone Resistance Band Heaters were necessary to ensure the barrel temperature within our specified thermal gradients and were therefore chosen as our key component for the Heating Mechanism.
