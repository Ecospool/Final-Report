Introduction

Motivation, objectives, scope and the broader context of polypropylene waste in Singapore and beyond.

1.1 Problem Statement

Our industry partner, NUS Sustainability Strategy Unit (NUS SSU), presented us with our initial problem statement: "What can we do with the current polypropylene (PP) waste in NUS instead of disposing of it for incineration?"

PP waste on campus is currently disposed of through incineration. SSU recognised that this is not an effective end-of-life solution and is seeking alternatives that could unlock value, reduce post-consumer PP waste on campus, and better utilise the abundant PP material.

Based on our interim, we established recycling as the most sustainable and value-generating approach for managing PP waste on campus. The next step was to identify which specific recycling pathway was best suited to the constraints and objectives of this project. Three candidate directions were evaluated: plastic sheets, pelletisation, and 3D printing filament production.

The solution must be operable at campus scale without requiring industrial-grade infrastructure. It must generate sufficient material value to justify the investment in recovery. It must be capable of retaining the recovered material within the campus ecosystem as required by NUS SSU, supporting a closed-loop system. And it should align with the demands of the NUS community, maximising the adoption of the recovered output.

When evaluated against the specific requirements of the problem statement, Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF) / 3D printing filament is the only pathway that simultaneously satisfies all four criteria: campus-scale operability, high material value-add, institutional closed-loop potential, and direct alignment with existing campus workflows. A more detailed breakdown of our thought process can be found in Appendix A.

The remainder of this report therefore focuses on the development of a system capable of producing rPP filament directly from post-consumer PP waste generated within NUS.

1.2 Project Objective

Our project aims to design and develop a modified extrusion system capable of producing usable post-consumer recycled polypropylene (PCW-PP) filament directly from shredded PP feedstock. The proposed approach eliminates the need for intermediate pelletisation, thereby reducing the thermal cycle and minimising material degradation for sustained recycling.

The work focuses on investigating and optimising key processing parameters, including screw compression ratio, heating, and cooling, through systematic experimentation and analysis. In addition, the project involves modifications to the extrusion system to achieve stable processing conditions and consistent filament quality.

The quality of the produced filament will be evaluated based on dimensional consistency, mechanical properties, and suitability for additive manufacturing applications.

Ultimately, this project directly addresses the challenge posed by NUS SSU by demonstrating the technical feasibility of converting post-consumer PP waste into filament for Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF). Rather than disposing of PP through incineration, the proposed system unlocks material value by transforming waste into a functional, directly usable filament that can be loaded into a 3D printer. It can be immediately applied to prototyping, design, and fabrication activities within NUS makerspaces and design spaces. In doing so, it reduces the volume of PP directed towards incineration and retains recovered material within the campus, moving PP waste management at NUS towards a closed-loop system.

1.3 Enabling a Circular Plastic Economy

Our proposed system is designed to reintegrate PP waste back into the production cycle as functional filament for FFF, directly challenging the conventional linear end-of-life pathway of collection and incineration. Rather than treating recycling as a downstream disposal option, this approach allows material recovery to be integrated directly into the institution's operational workflow.

The system operates on two levels of circularity. At the primary level, PP waste generated through ongoing campus activities can be continuously collected, shredded, and reprocessed into filament for immediate use within the same institution, closing the material loop locally without external recycling methods. At the secondary level, the accumulated PP waste without a recovery pathway can be processed through the system.

Together, these position the system as a scalable model for institutional plastic recovery, aligned with broader circular economy principles and NUS's sustainability objectives.

1.4 Scope & Constraints

With the project objective established, it is necessary to define the scope and constraints within which our system is designed and evaluated. These steer our design decisions made throughout the project. To ensure the system is fit for purpose across all relevant considerations, the scope is assessed across three dimensions: technical, economic, and operational.

Technical

Material consistency: Our system is focused purely on recovering waste PP. This allows for targeted optimisation of processing parameters, including heating profiles, screw compression, and cooling rates, specific to its thermal behaviour, enabling us to tightly control and reproduce experimentation.

Direct flake processing: Shredded flakes represent the most accessible feedstock format for this system, as they are the direct output of the PP waste stream requiring no further preparation before entering the extrusion stage. While pelletisation would produce a more geometrically uniform feedstock, it introduces an additional processing step that requires dedicated industrial equipment. By accepting flakes directly, the system eliminates this stage entirely, reducing the barrier to adoption and making the recovery process actionable within the constraints of the institution.

Elimination of an additional thermal cycle: By accepting flakes directly, the system avoids reheating the material a second time before extrusion, halving the thermal cycle required for reprocessing. Accepting flakes directly preserves the material in its least-degraded recoverable state, avoiding the molecular chain scission associated with an additional melt-processing cycle [1]. This is advantageous as it keeps the plastic in the recycling loop for twice as long.

Economic

Cost reduction: Eliminating pelletising equipment significantly lowers the upfront investment, thus reducing the cost of upcycling PP waste. Reduced investment in external processes makes the system easier to adopt within the institutional setting.

Operational cost efficiency: Removing one thermal cycle reduces energy consumption per kilogram of output, thereby lowering running costs in resource-limited environments.

Feedstock availability: PP is abundantly available as post-consumer waste in educational settings, commonly found in one-time laboratory packaging and consumable supplies, providing a readily accessible material source without external procurement.

Operational

Realistic preparation workflow: Shredding equipment is widely available in makerspaces and educational workshops, whereas pelletisers are more uncommon. Designing the system around flake feedstock aligns with the realistic material preparation of our intended user base.

Safety and manageability: Operating at laboratory scale with PP alone reduces operational risk and training requirements. This makes the system appropriate for supervised institutional use without the need for specialised technical operators.

Intra-institutional closed-loop operation: The system is designed to operate entirely within NUS to collect, process, and recover material without reliance on external supply chains or resources. PP waste generated on campus is shredded, extruded into filament, and consumed within the same institution, creating a self-contained material loop that is both operationally and logistically coherent. Nothing is brought in from outside, and nothing leaves the system as waste. The output is directly absorbed by the existing demand for 3D printing filament identified within the campus community.

1.5 Plastic Demand

To establish the practical demand of the proposed system, a three-part validation was conducted to assess the supply of PP waste available for recovery, the existing demand for 3D printing filament within the NUS community, and user acceptance to adopt PP as a printing material.

1.5.1 Supply of PP Waste

PP waste generated on campus through laboratory consumables, packaging, and disposable supplies represents a substantial and recurring feedstock source.

Preliminary figures from NUS SSU indicated that an average laboratory disposes of roughly 20 pipette tip boxes per week. Assuming approximately 300 laboratories across the institution and a weight of 107 grams per PP pipette box, the total amount of PP discarded reaches an estimated 33,384 kilograms per year.

It is also confirmed that these materials are currently directed towards incineration with no existing recovery pathway, establishing both the availability and accessibility of the feedstock required to supply the proposed system.

1.5.3 User Acceptance of PP as a Printing Material

Beyond quantifying demand, early-stage acceptance validation was initiated with architecture students, who have begun pre-testing virgin PP filament as a material option for their 3D printing activities. This first stage serves to establish user familiarity with PP as a printing material and identify any baseline compatibility considerations specific to their workflows. In subsequent stages, filament produced through our system will be introduced, enabling a comparison of print quality, usability, and dimensional consistency between virgin and recycled PP under the same real-world conditions. Feedback gathered across both stages will inform us on further refinements and serve as a proof-of-concept for the viability of rPP filament within NUS.

Taken together, these three validation streams confirm that there is available feedstock, demonstrated demand, and growing user acceptance.
