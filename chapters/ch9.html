A summary of the system, its implemented solutions, and the broader outlook for closed-loop PP recovery.

9. Conclusion

Our project set out to address a practical and pressing sustainability challenge posed by the NUS Sustainability Strategy Unit: the conversion of polypropylene (PP) waste into functional FFF filament, without intermediate pelletisation. What began as a material recovery problem evolved into a systematic engineering investigation, during which we designed, modified, and validated an extrusion system capable of producing printable PCW-PP filament directly from shredded PP flakes.

Revisiting the Objectives

The project's core objectives were to investigate and optimise the key parameters affecting filament quality, implement custom modifications to the extrusion system, and demonstrate the technical feasibility of the proposed approach.

All three objectives were addressed. Through independent subsystem investigations, the compression ratio, heating configuration, and cooling arrangement were systematically evaluated and optimised based on the specific material characteristics of the PP flake feedstock. The findings from each subsystem were consolidated into a final integrated prototype, the performance of which was validated through an end-to-end extrusion trial producing a continuous filament sample, which was assessed against predefined dimensional and functional quality thresholds.

Addressing the Key Issues

Two key issues of filament inconsistency were identified, and we proposed and implemented targeted solutions to address them (Fig 73).

A compression ratio of 3.5:1 was established as optimal for PP flake feedstock, providing sufficient mechanical compression to remove air bubbles and achieve melt seal without inducing the plugged feed zone conditions associated with excessive compression.

Multi-zone resistance-band heating enabled a controlled, graduated thermal profile along the barrel, eliminating the localised temperature gradients and thermal stratification that arise from single-zone configurations.

Our OmniFlow wind tunnel cooling addresses the anisotropic shrinkage problem by ensuring symmetric heat extraction around the filament cross-section, directly minimising ovality.

Together, these solutions reduced the two dominant sources of dimensional variation and produced a filament sufficiently consistent to be processed by a standard FDM 3D printer — validating our system's functional viability.

Where Our Innovation Lies

Existing small-scale extrusion systems treat recycled feedstock as an afterthought — adapting pellet-centric architectures to accommodate irregular flake material, inevitably at the expense of filament consistency. Our system is designed around the material characteristics and processing demands of post-consumer PP in flake form; the resulting configuration is not a compromise but a purpose-built solution.

The decision to bypass pelletisation entirely is not merely a process simplification but a deliberate choice that reduces thermal degradation, lowers equipment cost, and lengthens the material’s useful cycle. In doing so, this project demonstrates that consistent, printable filament can be produced from waste PP without the typical infrastructure required. This positions us as a viable model for closed-loop plastic recovery in educational and institutional settings.

Outlook

While the integrated trial confirmed the system's technical feasibility, there are still opportunities for future development. Quantitative characterisation of the produced filament — including tensile strength, elongation at break, and crystallinity — would provide a more complete mechanical profile for comparison against commercial benchmarks. Broader feedstock trials across varying PP waste streams would assess the system's robustness to the variability in other post-consumer materials. In the longer term, the deployment of this system within an active campus makerspace, as initiated through early-stage collaboration with the NUS Innovation and Design Hub, presents an opportunity to validate its real-world utility and refine the workflow for non-specialist users.

Ultimately, this project demonstrates that PP waste need not be an end-of-life problem. And with the right system design, it becomes an abundant resource with value.
